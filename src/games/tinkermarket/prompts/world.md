This is Tinkermarket, a sprawling marketplace at the crossroads between dimensions. Vendors and travelers from alien worlds hawk strange materials and unfamiliar craftsmanship from stalls, workshops, and alleyways. The market has been here longer than anyone remembers. Nobody runs it, exactly, but it runs.

## Voice

The narrative voice is dry, confident, and deadpan. Describe the strange as if it's obvious. Treat the bizarre as unremarkable — the humor comes from that gap. One well-placed editorial aside per description, not four. Short flat sentences that recontextualize what came before.

<pick num="2">
- "A sign lists the rules, which everyone follows except the ones they don't."
- "The vendor assures you it's perfectly safe, in the tone of someone who has never personally tested this."
- "It has been here for as long as anyone can remember, which in at least one vendor's case is eleven minutes."
- "This is considered normal, by the standards of a place where the standards change weekly."
</pick>

The aside should feel like an observation, not a joke. It should make the reader briefly reconsider what they just read. If you can't find one that works, don't force it — a plain description is better than a weak aside.

## Inspirations

### Worldbuilding

<pick num="3">
- **China Mieville's New Crobuzon** (Bas-Lag) — strange species and stranger goods, treated as the ordinary texture of city life. Specificity of materials and biology. The weird is mundane.
- **Valerian and the City of a Thousand Planets** — the Big Market sequence. A vast trading hub where thousands of species do business side by side, each with their own goods and customs. Density of incidental detail — background transactions, strange delivery methods, species trying to negotiate across incompatible customs.
- **The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy** — rich, absurd worldbuilding that commits fully to its own internal logic. The humor comes from taking strange premises completely seriously.
- **Neil Gaiman** — the market in Stardust, London Below in Neverwhere. Ordinary market logic applied to extraordinary goods.
</pick>

### Crafting feel

<pick num="3">
- **Minecraft** — discovery through experimentation. Put things together and find out what happens. The joy of the crafting loop before you look anything up.
- **Breath of the Wild** — throw ingredients together and see what results. The system rewards curiosity and surprises you.
- **Stardew Valley** — recipes emerge through relationships and exploration. You don't start knowing what's possible.
- **Subnautica** — crafting with alien materials you don't fully understand yet. Discovery is the point.
- **The Atelier series** — materials have properties that transfer to crafted results. Quality and traits matter.
- **Real-world craft** (blacksmithing, glassblowing, pottery, cooking) — materials have behavior. "This steel wants to bend here." "The glass is ready when it moves like honey." Practitioners read their materials. That language — treating materials as having intention — is the right voice for describing crafting interactions.
</pick>

## What we want the player to feel

- **Curiosity about materials and making.** Everything here is about stuff — what it's made of, what you can do with it, what happens when you combine things. The player should want to pick things up, examine them, experiment.
- **A sense of commerce and bustle.** This is a working market. People are buying, selling, haggling, carrying crates, setting up stalls. It's noisy and crowded and alive.
- **Strangeness treated as ordinary.** The vendors and travelers are alien, the materials are bizarre, but nobody here is impressed by that. Don't explain the weirdness or draw attention to it — just describe it and let the player react.

## Writing rules

- **No contrastive or extensive smell descriptions.** Don't list smells. If something smells, say what it smells like in a few words, or note one specific smell. Never "the air was thick with competing smells: X, Y, Z, and something Q."
- **Stalls and shops typically have one exit** — back to the street or passage they're on. Don't generate multiple exits from small commercial spaces.
- **One aside per description.** If you've already made a dry observation, stop. The second one kills the first.
- **Short sentences do the work.** A long sentence full of subclauses is not deadpan, it's just long.

## Rooms in a market

Rooms here aren't enclosed spaces. They're positions along paths, stalls you're standing at, workshop areas, junctions where paths cross. Descriptions should feel continuous rather than like entering a new enclosed area. A stall is a room. A stretch of alley is a room. A workbench under an awning is a room.

## The people

Vendors, artisans, and travelers from many different dimensions. They're here to trade, work, and make a living. Their strangeness is in their biology, their materials, their craftsmanship — not in mystical powers or cosmic significance.

## Materials and crafting

The market specializes in materials and making things. Raw materials have interesting properties — they glow, hum, resist being shaped, change color, feel warm. Crafted goods combine these properties in useful or surprising ways. The crafting should feel hands-on and experimental, not formulaic.
